5.1 Perpandicularity Proof¶

Set $b=0$, we can get $<\mathbf{w}, \mathbf{x}> = 0$, in this case $\mathbf{w} \perp \mathbf{x}$. We can further translate $\mathbf{x}$ by an arbitrary distance $\mathbf{a}$, because this is just a pure translation, we get $<\mathbf{w}, (\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{a})> = 0$, alternatively $$ \mathbf{w}^\top (\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{a}) = 0 \\ \Rightarrow ~ \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{a} = 0 , $$ for that $\mathbf{a}$ is arbitrary, we can let $b = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{a}$, the result is still a translation of $\mathbf{x}$, so the relationship holds $\mathbf{w} \perp \mathbf{x}$

5.2 Distance¶

Follow the steps in 5.1 and w.l.o.g. let $\mathbf{a}$ perpandicular to the hyperplane, which is the shortest vector between $\mathbf{w}$ and $\mathbf{x}$, the distance can be calculated as $\text{distance} = \left\| \mathbf{a} \right\|$, i.e.,

$$ \left\| \mathbf{a} \right\| = \frac{\left\| \mathbf{b} \right\|}{\left\| \mathbf{w} \right\|} $$

  1. A: person is sick
  2. B: test result is positive
  3. C: person is health

(a)¶

To be found is $P(B)$

$$ \begin{align*} P{B} &= P(B|A)P(A)+P(B|C)P(C) \\ &= 0.005 \times 0.95 + (1-0.005) \times 0.1 \\ &\approx 10\% \end{align*} $$

(b)¶

To be found is $P(A|B)$, we can utilize Bayes' rule

$$ \begin{align*} P{A|B} &= \frac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)} \\ &= \frac{0.95 \times 0.005}{0.1} \\ &\approx 4\% \end{align*} $$

(c)¶

To be found is $P(C|D)$

$$ \begin{align*} P(C|D) &= 1-P(A|D) \\ & = 1 - \frac{P(D|A)P(A)}{P(D)} & = 1 - \frac{(1-P(B|A))P(A)}{1-P(B)} &\approx 99\% \end{align*} $$

7.1 Global Minimum of Convex Function¶

Let $\mathbf{x}^*$ be the local minimum of $f$, assume it is not the global minimum, which meams there exist a $\mathbf{x}_0$ such that $f(\mathbf{x}_0) < f(\mathbf{x}^*)$.

By definition, for a convex function, for any $\theta \in (0,1)$ we have

$$ \begin{align*} f(\theta \mathbf{x}^* + (1-\theta)\mathbf{x}_0) &\le \theta f(\mathbf{x}^*) + (1-\theta)f(\mathbf{x}_0) \\ &< \theta f(\mathbf{x}^*) + (1-\theta)f(\mathbf{x}^*)\\ \Rightarrow f(\theta \mathbf{x}^* + (1-\theta)\mathbf{x}_0) &< f(\mathbf{x}^*) \end{align*} $$

Now, if $\theta\to 1$, $\theta \mathbf{x}^* + (1-\theta)\mathbf{x}_0 \to \mathbf{x}^*$, in such case, $\mathbf{x}^*$ cannot be the local minimum neither.

Therefore local minimun $\mathbf{x}^*$ of a convex function must also be the global minimum.
